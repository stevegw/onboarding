01 — The Starting Point

Good documentation.
Hard to learn from.

As someone who has worked in enablement for a long time, I've seen how users actually relate to technical documentation. With Windchill, the pattern was familiar: the content is thorough and accurate, but it's structured for people who already understand the system — not for people still building that understanding.

The web help covers features in isolation. Topics rarely explain how they connect to other parts of the system, or what a user is actually trying to achieve when they land on that page. The result is documentation that answers precise questions well, but doesn't help someone develop working knowledge of how Windchill fits together.

This is the gap I wanted to explore using AI — not to replace the documentation, but to see whether AI could help bridge the distance between reference material and genuine understanding.

A note on the approach: I was the only person running these experiments. Being able to evaluate each result against experience of what good enablement looks like — what flows, what confuses, what learners actually need — was central to how I assessed what was and wasn't working.

02 — The Web Scraping Experiments

An idea that seemed right — and what it actually taught me

My first instinct was to use the web help pages themselves as the raw material. Scraping them programmatically felt like an elegant solution — the content was already there, AI could restructure it into something more learnable. I tried this three times, each version a response to what the previous one revealed.

Version 1
Basic scraping + AI restructuring

What I tried

Scraped Windchill web help pages and passed the raw content through the Claude API to generate scenario-based challenge cards — each with a question, context, progressive hints, and an answer.

What I found

  • The pipeline worked technically and produced content quickly.
  • But the output quality was inconsistent. Questions were sometimes off-target, answers occasionally missed the mark.
  • The web help's reference-style writing didn't give AI enough context to generate reliably useful learning material.
Version 2
Refined prompting + local LLM

What I tried

Refined the approach and introduced a local LLM (Ollama/Mistral) to explore whether a more dynamic, on-device model could improve adaptability and reduce dependency on external APIs. This was also a test of the concept of locally-run AI for enablement.

What I found

  • The local LLM experiment was genuinely useful — it confirmed that dynamic AI-generated learning is technically feasible.
  • However, output quality with smaller local models was noticeably lower than with Claude.
  • The concept of dynamic content generation was validated even if this specific path got too abstract to pursue further.
Version 3
Further iteration on scraping

What I tried

A third pass at refining the scraping and generation pipeline, attempting to address the quality and consistency issues through better processing of the scraped content before passing it to AI.

What I found

  • Incremental improvement, but the fundamental problem remained.
  • The source material was the limiting factor, not the pipeline or the prompting.
  • No amount of refinement could fully compensate for web help content that lacked the depth and context needed for good learning material.

Three attempts at web scraping taught me something I might not have arrived at another way: the quality of the output is determined almost entirely by the quality of the input. The scraping approach wasn't a failure — it was a necessary experiment that pointed clearly to where I needed to go next.

03 — The Content Quality Pivot

When the source improved,
everything improved.

Moving to official PTC PDF documentation as the source material changed the results noticeably. PDFs contain more complete explanations, proper context, and content that had been written with some thought for the reader. That gave AI something genuinely useful to work with.

At the same time, I shifted focus from generating quizzes toward building interactive learning guides — content structured around how a user thinks through a Windchill task, not how the documentation is organised. The difference in how that landed was clear.

📋
PDF as the source of truth
Official Windchill and Codebeamer PDFs gave AI enough context to produce accurate, complete learning content — not just structurally reformatted reference text.
🔁
Workflow order, not feature order
Guides built around what a user is trying to do — rather than mirroring documentation structure — felt immediately more useful and easier to follow.
🎯
Learning flow matters
Interactive HTML guides on real topics (Change Implementation, Access Control, role-permission matrices) got a stronger response — because they had a beginning, middle, and end that built understanding progressively.
🔓
No IT dependency by design
Platform choices throughout were deliberately low-friction — browser-based, no login, no LMS infrastructure required. The goal was accessibility without organisational overhead.
The difference in practice
Same topic. Very different learning experience.
Starting from web help
  • Reference language without context
  • Isolated topic with no workflow connection
  • Requires the reader to already know why they're there
  • AI output inconsistent — some questions miss the mark
Starting from verified PDF
  • Complete explanations with proper context
  • AI can reason about the topic accurately
  • Learning material follows a logical progression
  • Content feels authored, not generated

On platform choice: Throughout the exploration I deliberately avoided solutions that would require IT involvement or infrastructure decisions. The aim was to test ideas quickly and accessibly — to understand what works before asking anyone else to commit to it.

04 — What I've Concluded

AI works best when it works
alongside human expertise —
not instead of it.

Finding 01
Source quality is everything
The scraping experiments made this undeniable. AI amplifies what's in the source material — if the source is shallow or context-free, so is the output. Verified, well-written documentation produces genuinely useful learning content.
Finding 02
Dynamic AI-generated learning is feasible
The local LLM experiment confirmed the concept works, even if that specific path wasn't the right one to pursue. AI can generate responsive, contextual learning content — the question is how to do it reliably and at acceptable quality.
Finding 03
The enabler's role doesn't disappear
Experienced judgement was essential at every stage — recognising when output was off-target, understanding why, and knowing what good learning design looks like. AI accelerates the work; it doesn't replace the expertise behind it.
Finding 04
Flow and accessibility matter as much as accuracy
Technically correct content that doesn't flow, or that lives in a place users won't find it, doesn't achieve anything. The delivery and the design of the learning experience matter just as much as what's in it.
The central conclusion

The most interesting possibility isn't AI replacing content creation — it's AI and human content expertise working in harmony. The human brings validated knowledge, learning design, and judgement. AI brings scale, accessibility, and the ability to make that knowledge findable at the moment it's needed.

On the broader question

This exploration was genuinely about understanding where AI fits — not proving that it does. What it's shown me is that the value isn't in automating enablement away. It's in using AI to extend what a skilled enabler can do: turning good source material into learning experiences that reach more people, more often, with less friction. What happens with that understanding is a question for the organisation to answer.

05 — Questions Worth Asking Next

The exploration surfaced
some useful open questions

These aren't recommendations — they're honest questions that follow from what the experiments revealed. Where the organisation wants to take them is a separate conversation.

💬
Where do users go today when they're stuck in Windchill? Understanding this current behaviour is essential before deciding how or whether to change it.
📋
Which PTC PDFs would form the most useful validated knowledge base? The source selection matters enormously — not all documentation is equally suitable as learning material.
🤝
How should content creators and AI tools work together in practice? The experimentation suggests there's a productive model here — but it needs to be designed deliberately, not assumed.
🔗
What's the right delivery channel for our users? The mechanism matters less than meeting people where they already are — but that means understanding where that actually is.
🔄
How does the knowledge base stay current? Any AI-supported learning solution is only as good as its sources. Who owns keeping those sources accurate and up to date?
